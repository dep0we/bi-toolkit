# Data Narrative Builder

Used in the assay lifecycle at: Stage 9 (shared package and deliver)

## Quick Start

Transform validated findings into a clear data narrative, meaning a story built from evidence, that helps the audience understand what changed, why it matters, and what action is needed. Do not make the narrative stronger than the evidence.

## Context Requirements

1. **Data or analysis**: validated findings and supporting evidence.
2. **Audience**: who will read or hear it.
3. **Goal**: what action or understanding is needed.
4. **Format**: presentation, report, email, dashboard note, or executive summary.
5. **Constraints**: time, length, sensitivity, and formality.
6. **Confidence and limitations**: what is known, uncertain, or out of scope.

## Context Gathering

"To build the narrative, I need:

- Central insight.
- Audience and what they care about.
- Decision or action requested.
- Delivery format.
- Evidence that has passed validation.
- Sensitive topics or risks to avoid overstating.
- Limitations that must be disclosed."

## Workflow

### Step 1: Choose Narrative Structure

Select a structure by consequence:

- Situation, complication, resolution: best for problem and recommendation.
- Before, after, bridge: best for impact of a change.
- What is, what could be, call to action: best for strategic choice.
- Status, drivers, next steps: best for recurring business reviews.

Use the simplest structure that gets the audience to the decision.

### Step 2: Build the Evidence Spine

List the validated claims in order:

1. Baseline.
2. Change or surprise.
3. Driver or likely explanation.
4. Business impact.
5. Recommended action.

For each claim, attach the supporting number, source, and limitation. If a claim does not have support, weaken it or remove it.

### Step 3: Weave in Data Points

Use numbers where they change understanding. Prefer:

- Rounded values for recall.
- Comparisons to baseline.
- Time context.
- Human or financial consequence.

Define terms inline, such as median (middle value, ignores outliers), correlation (things moving together), or churn (customers or revenue lost).

### Step 4: Plan Visual Storytelling

Map visuals to the story:

- Baseline: trend or benchmark chart.
- Change: before/after chart.
- Driver: breakdown or segment comparison.
- Action: roadmap, scenario, or expected impact.

Every visual should answer one question. Remove visuals that only decorate.

### Step 5: Control Pacing

Open with the conclusion or the stakes, then move through evidence. Keep method details in appendix unless the method affects trust. Use plain-language signposts so the audience knows what each section means for the decision.

### Step 6: Write the Call to Action

End with:

- Decision needed.
- Owner.
- Timeline.
- Trade-off.
- Success criteria.
- Follow-up review.

If uncertainty is material, state what action is safe despite uncertainty or what validation is needed before acting.

## Context Validation

- [ ] Narrative has a clear beginning, middle, and end.
- [ ] Data supports the story and does not overwhelm it.
- [ ] Audience perspective is considered.
- [ ] Confidence and limitations are included.
- [ ] Call to action is specific.
- [ ] Visuals clarify the decision.

## Output Template

```text
# [Title that states the business issue]

## Bottom Line
[One or two sentences: finding, confidence, action]

## What Changed
[Baseline and change, with validated numbers]

## Why It Matters
[Business consequence]

## What We Found
1. [finding] - [evidence] - [meaning]
2. [finding] - [evidence] - [meaning]

## What We Recommend
1. [action] - [expected outcome] - [owner/timing]

## Confidence and Limits
- Confidence: [high/moderate/low and why]
- Limits: [what the analysis does not prove]

## Decision Needed
[specific ask, deadline, and next step]
```

## Common Scenarios

- Skeptical audience: lead with their concern, show counterevidence, and recommend a low-risk next step.
- Monthly metrics: identify what changed, why it matters, and what needs action.
- Complex analysis: start with the answer, define technical terms inline, and move method detail to appendix.
- Dashboard commentary: explain movement, driver, confidence, and next action.
